Research

In our research, we use computational chemistry in two primary projects:

  • Explore the fluorescence of carbon dots. Carbon dots were an accidental discovery, but they are attractive to scientists because of their ability to fluoresce in every color and their potential bio-compatibility. Their properties give carbon dots important applications in forensics analysis and in bioimaging. A question stands regarding the reason for why carbon dots fluoresce - in particular, many results point toward blue fluorescence, but experimentally observed red fluorescence has not been fully explained yet. A combination of computational methods can help us answer that question by investigating the structural properties of carbon dots that may result in red fluorescence!
  • Explain the nature of bonding. Covalent bonding is understood to be due to the sharing of electrons between two atoms and can often be readily described with Lewis structures. However, every now and then some weird molecules are observed that make us question how far can the general chemistry rules be broken (for an example, consider rhodium monoboride, which is subject to an active debate whether it is triple- or quadruple-bonded). Using very rigorous theoretical methods, we can develop a 3D, orbital-based view of what bonding looks like in those weird molecules to explain what orbitals participate in bonding, how hybridization occurs, and what are the energetics of bond formation.
